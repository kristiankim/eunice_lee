## What kind of therapy do you offer?

The source describes an integrative approach for adults that draws from Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), Motivational Enhancement Therapy, psychodynamic psychotherapy, and a contemporary relational psychoanalytic perspective.

This approach considers both what is happening in a client’s life now and what emerges in the relationship between client and therapist. Those interactions may offer useful insight into patterns that also appear in the client’s other relationships.

## Is your approach brief or long-term?

The source offers both short- and long-term therapy depending on each client’s goals and needs.

Short-term work usually concentrates on a particular concern. It tends to be more structured and may involve learning new concepts, identifying unhelpful thinking patterns, completing exercises, and practicing new skills.

Long-term work is generally less structured and allows time to explore broader parts of a client’s life. It may use some of the same exercises as short-term therapy while giving more attention to the larger picture. Although the present remains important, past experiences may also be explored to understand how they influence current life.

## How long does treatment typically last?

The source explains that the length of therapy is tailored to each client. It characterizes short-term work as commonly lasting approximately 10–20 sessions and longer-term work as more open-ended, often with a commitment of at least one year.

## How often would we meet?

The source says that session frequency is decided collaboratively, based on the therapist’s recommendation and the client’s needs and preferences.

Weekly meetings are presented as standard for short-term work because they allow clients to practice strategies between sessions. The source also says long-term therapy may take place weekly, while some clients choose to meet two or three times per week.

## Can I bring someone to my sessions?

The source primarily describes individual therapy. In some circumstances, a client may arrange for a significant other to attend part of the work or meet with the therapist separately. Any such involvement would be discussed and planned in advance.
