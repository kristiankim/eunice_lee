## Overall Impression

The homepage earns trust quickly and handles vulnerable attention with unusual care. Its single biggest opportunity is to stop presenting Eunice as a complete catalogue of therapy services and start making her specific way of listening, communicating, and supporting bilingual clients the organizing idea. The current middle loses the human connection established by the hero.

## What's Working

1. **Concrete credibility:** Licensure numbers, education, experience, languages, location, and session lengths are stronger than vague wellness claims.
2. **Low-pressure copy:** “You don’t need to have everything figured out” and “No obligation to continue” reduce perceived commitment without urgency tactics.
3. **Coherent visual system:** Square edges, hairlines, committed olive surfaces, real photography, accessible focus treatment, and reduced-motion support fit a professional private practice.

## Priority Issues

### [P1] The identity is still too close to the therapy-site default

**Why it matters:** The design is credible but not yet memorable as Eunice Lee. Visitors may trust it without retaining anything uniquely hers.

**Fix:** Preserve the olive and photography, but build one unmistakably personal art-direction idea around Eunice’s manner, bilingual perspective, office, or the line “Care that begins with listening.” Reduce the repeated label-heading-copy grammar and the Newsreader/DM Sans editorial-wellness feel.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable bolder homepage identity`

### [P1] Insurance language overpromises relative to the available detail

**Why it matters:** Cost is high-stakes. “Insurance accepted” and “Most major insurance plans” can be read as universal coverage when carriers, out-of-network status, and verification timing remain unknown.

**Fix:** Use “Insurance coverage varies by plan,” name only confirmed carriers, and explain when benefits are verified. Do not require an inquiry merely to decode a prominent trust claim.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable clarify homepage insurance copy`

### [P2] Static support rows falsely signal interactivity

**Why it matters:** Seven trailing arrows use the same directional language as clickable CTAs, but the rows are inert.

**Fix:** Remove the arrows and present the rows as editorial content, or make each row genuinely expandable or linked.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable polish support areas`

### [P2] The middle becomes an inventory instead of a relationship

**Why it matters:** Seven concerns, three steps, and eight acronyms create scan fatigue and describe Eunice’s toolkit more than the client experience.

**Fix:** Prioritize three or four human concerns, group the remainder, and replace the acronym cloud with a short explanation of how sessions feel and how methods are chosen.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable distill homepage middle sections`

### [P2] The ending asks twice instead of landing once

**Why it matters:** The olive closing section already resolves the journey. A second headline and CTA in the photographic footer makes a low-pressure site feel funnel-like.

**Fix:** Keep one decisive final CTA. Let the photographic footer provide atmosphere, credentials, address, and navigation without restarting the pitch.

**Suggested command:** `$impeccable distill homepage ending`

## Persona Red Flags

**Jordan — first-time therapy seeker**

- Clinical credentials and therapy acronyms are not explained where encountered.
- Support arrows promise unavailable detail.
- “Insurance accepted” sounds definitive while the useful answer is deferred.
- The page does not explain what the free consultation includes, how soon Eunice replies, or how fit is assessed.

**Casey — distracted mobile visitor**

- The full mobile journey is long and inventory-heavy.
- Insurance and location require substantial scrolling, with no in-page shortcuts.
- Portrait and footer imagery are not explicitly optimized for loading behavior.
- A visitor who decides midway through the page has no persistent thumb-zone action.

**Prospective client uncertain about therapy**

- The interface demonstrates competence more strongly than interpersonal fit.
- “Collaborative” and “client-centered” are asserted without concrete examples of Eunice’s manner.
- Korean-language care appears as a badge rather than a meaningful dimension of access.
- There is no explicit reassurance for “I’m not sure my concern belongs here.”

## Minor Observations

- CTA verbs shift among Book, Request, Ask, and Send for the same inquiry destination.
- “Book a consultation” implies direct scheduling, but the destination is an inquiry form.
- Repeated eyebrow labels remain visibly templated; `card-kicker` also reintroduces uppercase tracking.
- The footer’s 760px minimum height turns secondary site furniture into another major landing section.
- The portrait’s declared 320×400 dimensions do not exactly match the CSS frame ratio.
- English and Korean availability should not imply that translated site content exists.

## Questions to Consider

- If the olive palette, portrait, credentials, and name disappeared, what remaining element could only belong to Eunice?
- Could “Care that begins with listening” organize the whole page instead of appearing only as a portrait note?
- Should bilingual care meaningfully shape the experience, or remain a qualification?
- Which ending should visitors remember: the brief-conversation invitation or the photographic footer?
